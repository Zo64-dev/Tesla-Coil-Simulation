📓 Lab Notes

  • Resonance: the secondary coil rings like a tuning fork. Only when its natural frequency matches the primary's pulses (100 % tuning) does energy pile up into huge voltages — watch the arcs surge.
  • Ionization: the coil's field rips electrons off gas atoms in the bulb. When they recombine, each gas emits its own signature color — no wires needed.
  • Inverse-square law: field strength falls with distance squared. Halving the bulb's distance roughly quadruples the field.

⚡ How to Use This Lab

1 · Fire up the coil

Raise the Input Voltage and slide Secondary Tuning toward 100 %. A Tesla coil is a resonant transformer: like pushing a swing at exactly the right rhythm, energy only builds when the timing matches. Off-resonance, even high voltage produces feeble sparks.

2 · Adjust the spark gap

The Spark Gap Frequency sets how many times per second the primary circuit dumps its stored charge — you'll see the arcs snap and reform faster or slower.

3 · Bring in the gas bulb

Drag the bulb toward the toroid (or use the distance slider). The coil's electromagnetic field ionizes the gas — stripping electrons from atoms. As electrons fall back into place, they release photons at wavelengths unique to each element: neon glows red-orange, argon lavender, xenon deep blue. This is the same physics behind neon signs and spectroscopy.

4 · Compare gases

Xenon (12.1 eV) ionizes far more easily than helium (24.6 eV) — swap gases at the same distance and watch which lights first. The field readout follows the inverse-square law, so small changes in distance make a big difference.
