📓 Lab Notes

  • Lenz's law: induced currents always flow in the direction that opposes the change creating them. The tube fights the magnet's arrival below it and mourns its departure above — both effects push upward.
  • No contact, real force: the magnet never touches the tube. The braking is pure field-on-field interaction — the same physics behind maglev train brakes and induction cooktops.
  • The slotted tube secret: eddy currents must flow in closed rings around the tube. Cut one slot down its length and the rings can't complete — same copper, no brake. Geometry is everything.

🧲 How to Use This Lab

Gravity-defying — almost

Drop a strong magnet down a copper pipe and it doesn't fall. It descends — drifting down in eerie slow motion as if the air were syrup. Nothing touches it. This lab lets you race that magnet against an identical one in a glass tube, and see exactly why one wins by seconds.

1 · Drop and watch the rings

As the magnet moves, its field sweeps through the metal, and the metal answers: swirling loops of current — eddy currents — spin up in the tube walls. By Lenz's law they always oppose the change: the ring below the magnet repels it, the ring above attracts it back. Both push up. The glowing loops in the tube show them live, brightening with speed.

2 · Change the material and the magnet

Braking strength scales with the tube's conductivity and with the magnet's field squared. Copper brakes hard, aluminum moderately, plastic not at all. Swap the N52 neodymium for a ceramic magnet and the brake nearly vanishes — same tube, ten times weaker grip.

3 · Follow the energy

Here's the profound part: the magnet loses kinetic energy, but energy is never destroyed. Watch the heat meter — every joule of "missing" motion reappears as warmth in the tube walls, dissipated by those swirling currents. The slow fall isn't magic; it's the universe keeping its books balanced.
