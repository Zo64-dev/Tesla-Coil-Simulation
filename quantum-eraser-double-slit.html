📓 Lab Notes

  • Each photon interferes with itself: even fired one at a time, with the chamber empty, single photons land in stripes. The wave that interferes is a wave of probability — passing through both slits at once.
  • The observer effect: it's not that looking "disturbs" the photon with a clumsy nudge. Gaining which-way information — anywhere, by any means — is itself what destroys the interference. Information is physical.
  • The eraser: scramble that information after the slits and the stripes return. The universe only insists: you may have the path or the pattern — never both.

🌊 How to Use This Lab

Feynman called it "the only mystery"

Fire light at a wall with two narrow slits, and stripes appear on the screen behind — waves from each slit adding and canceling. Fine, light is a wave. But this lab fires one photon at a time, and the stripes still form, dot by dot. Each photon somehow goes through both slits and interferes with itself.

1 · Build the impossible pattern

Set the Firing Rate to 1/s and watch. Each photon arrives as a single point — particle behavior — yet after a few hundred, unmistakable stripes emerge — wave behavior. The histogram beside the screen tallies the counts against the theoretical curve.

2 · Look — and destroy it

Flip the "Which-Way" Detector ON. Now each photon's slit is recorded (see the readout: TOP or BOTTOM). Instantly, the stripes stop forming — new photons pile into two plain bands, exactly as everyday particles would. Nothing physical touched the photons differently. The universe simply behaves differently when the answer to "which slit?" exists.

3 · Un-look with the eraser

With the detector still on, enable the Quantum Eraser — a device that scrambles the which-way record after the slits, so the information can never be recovered. The stripes return. Path or pattern: the choice is yours, but never both. That trade-off — not lasers, not slits — is the deep law this experiment exposes.
