📓 Lab Notes

  • Induction: a charged ring doesn't touch the water — it pulls opposite charge into each drop as it breaks free. The drop then carries that charge down into the can.
  • Positive feedback: the wires cross — each can charges the ring above the other stream. More charge → stronger induction → more charge. A whisper becomes a shout.
  • Breakdown: when the field between the electrodes exceeds ~3 kV/mm, air itself ionizes and a spark dumps everything at once.

💧 How to Use This Lab

The most improbable machine in physics

In 1867, Lord Kelvin built a generator with no moving parts except falling water — yet it produces tens of thousands of volts. Two streams drip from one reservoir through two metal rings into two cans. That's it. That's the whole machine.

1 · Watch the whisper become a shout

By pure chance, one side always starts with a few extra electrons. Because the wires cross — each can feeds the ring above the opposite stream — that tiny imbalance amplifies itself. Watch the drops change color as they pick up charge, and the voltage curve bend upward exponentially.

2 · Race growth against leakage

Raise Drip Rate and Drop Size to charge faster. Raise Humidity and charge bleeds away through the damp air — push it high enough and the machine loses the race entirely. This is why the real experiment fails on rainy days.

3 · Set the spark gap

Air insulates until the field reaches about 3 kV per millimeter — then it ionizes and CRACK: the stored energy dumps in a flash and the cycle starts over. A wider gap means longer waits but bigger lightning.
