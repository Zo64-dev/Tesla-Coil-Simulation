📓 Lab Notes

  • How trails form: a charged particle rips electrons off air molecules as it flies. Supersaturated alcohol vapor condenses on those ions instantly — a contrail of mist marking where something invisible just passed.
  • Lorentz force: a magnetic field pushes moving charges sideways (F = qv×B). Positive and negative particles curl in opposite directions — this is literally how antimatter was discovered in 1932.
  • Mass matters: an alpha particle is ~7,300× heavier than an electron, so the same field barely nudges it while electrons corkscrew. (Curvature here is gently exaggerated so you can see it.)

🌫 How to Use This Lab

The window into the invisible

Right now, several cosmic ray particles are passing through your body every second. You can't feel them — but in 1911, C.T.R. Wilson built a chamber of supersaturated vapor where each one leaves a visible mist trail, like a jet contrail in miniature. It won him the Nobel Prize and gave physicists their first look at the subatomic world.

1 · Learn to read the tracks

Each particle has a signature. Alpha particles (helium nuclei) are heavy bulldozers — thick, straight, short trails. Beta particles (electrons) are pinballs — thin, jittery zig-zags as they bounce off atoms. Gamma rays are ghosts — uncharged, they leave no trail themselves, only faint wisps where they knock an electron loose.

2 · Switch on the magnet

Slide the Magnetic Field up and the chamber transforms: every moving charge feels the Lorentz force and its path bends into an arc. Positive charges curl one way, negative the other — flip the field direction and both reverse. Notice how lightweight electrons wind into tight spirals while heavy alphas barely lean.

3 · Meet the cosmic rain

Leave the cosmic ray background on and watch long, arrow-straight trails streak down from the top — muons born when cosmic rays smash into the upper atmosphere, reaching your screen at nearly the speed of light. This is the actual, permanent radiation environment of planet Earth.
