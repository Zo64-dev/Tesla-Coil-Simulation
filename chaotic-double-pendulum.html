📓 Lab Notes

  • Deterministic ≠ predictable: the equations here contain no dice rolls. Same start, same path, forever. Yet an error of 0.001° in knowing the start makes the future unknowable within seconds.
  • Sensitive dependence: tiny differences grow exponentially — the famous "butterfly effect." This is why weather forecasts decay after about a week no matter how good the computers get.
  • One hinge is all it takes: a single pendulum is solvable with high-school math. Adding one joint creates a system no formula on Earth can solve — only simulate.

🌀 How to Use This Lab

The most famous troublemaker in physics

A single pendulum is the picture of predictability — clocks were built on it for centuries. Hang a second pendulum from the tip of the first, and that predictability shatters. Nothing random was added. Just one extra hinge.

1 · Watch the twins

With Dual Overlay on, two pendulums launch with starting angles just 0.001° apart — far less than any real measurement could detect. For several seconds they move as one, pink hiding teal. Then, without warning, they tear apart into utterly different destinies. Watch the log chart: the separation climbs in a straight line, meaning it doubles again and again at a steady rhythm.

2 · Carve light with the tail

The Trace Tail slider draws the lower tip's path in neon. Notice the shape never repeats and never crosses itself the same way twice — you are watching a system explore its possibilities without ever settling down.

3 · Prove it's not random

Hit Restart repeatedly without changing anything: the motion replays identically, and the energy readout never drifts. The unpredictability isn't in the system — it's in our inability to know the starting point perfectly. That gap between determinism and predictability is the heart of chaos theory, and it's why weather, orbits of asteroids, and hearts in fibrillation all resist long-term forecasting.
